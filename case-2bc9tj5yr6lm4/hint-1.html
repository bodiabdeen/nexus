Evidence to Reexamine

Your investigation has uncovered substantial evidence, but have you noticed what's missing from certain critical moments?

Consider this: When analyzing the timeline reconstruction, pay very close attention to the exact timestamps of key events. Sometimes what appears to be a coincidence might actually be something more.

In particular, look carefully at the security logs between 09:20 AM and 09:50 AM. Notice who was where, and more importantly, notice who wasn't somewhere they should have been.

üîç Question to Consider:

One of the suspects has an extremely unusual timing pattern in the security logs. Their exit from a particular location occurs at the exact same minute as a critical event. This level of precision seems almost impossible as a coincidence.

Ask yourself: Is this suspect's timeline physically possible? Could one person have done everything the evidence suggests happened during that narrow window?

Consider the Phone Records

The phone records reveal some interesting late-night communications. When someone makes calls to a coworker at unusual hours (like 9:45 PM or later), it's worth asking: what were they discussing?

Pay attention to the content of text messages sent the night before the murder. Words like "take control of your own situation" might mean different things in different contexts.

Also notice: which suspect has two phones? And who did they call immediately after the body was discovered?

üîç Question to Consider:

If someone needed help committing a crime that required multiple capabilities (chemical knowledge, computer skills, and physical access), who would make the most logical partners? Look for connections between suspects - shared conversations, complementary skill sets, or matching motives.

The Computer Access Problem

The forensic report tells us that Project Helix files were stolen at 09:23 AM using Dr. Voss's credentials and biometric fingerprint. The forensic timeline also suggests she was unconscious or dying at that exact moment.

This raises an important question: Who had the technical knowledge, the security clearance level, and the physical access to accomplish this?

Review the case file carefully. Look at each suspect's security clearance level. Remember: Level 3 clearance is NOT sufficient for accessing secure research terminals. You need Level 4 or higher.

üîç Final Thought:

Not every suspect who looks guilty actually is guilty. Red herrings are placed deliberately. But sometimes two suspects who individually couldn't have committed the crime might have been able to do it together.

Look for evidence of collaboration. Did any two suspects communicate with each other around the time of the murder? Did they have complementary capabilities that would allow them to overcome each other's limitations?
